Speech Frameworks

Frameworks give your impromptu responses instant structure. Even when you don't know the topic well, a framework makes you sound organized and credible. Click any framework to learn more.

PREP

Best for: opinions, recommendations, answering direct questions
1 Point
2 Reason
3 Example
4 Point (restate)
How to use PREP:
Point: State your main takeaway up front. "I believe X."
Reason: Give the why. "The reason is..."
Example: Make it concrete. A story, data point, or analogy.
Point: Circle back to reinforce. "That's why X matters."

This is the single most versatile framework. When in doubt, use PREP.

What / So What / Now What

Best for: briefings, status updates, presenting findings
1 What
2 So What
3 Now What
How to use What / So What / Now What:
What: State the facts. What happened? What did you find?
So What: Why does it matter? What are the implications?
Now What: What should we do next? The call to action.

Perfect for sounding executive-level. Forces you to move beyond facts into insight.

Past / Present / Future

Best for: trend analysis, history questions, vision-casting
1 Past
2 Present
3 Future
How to use Past / Present / Future:
Past: Where did we come from? Historical context.
Present: Where are we now? Current state.
Future: Where are we headed? Your prediction or recommendation.

Great for sounding knowledgeable even on unfamiliar topics — everyone can anchor to "how things used to be."

Problem / Solution / Benefit

Best for: pitches, proposals, selling an idea
1 Problem
2 Solution
3 Benefit
How to use Problem / Solution / Benefit:
Problem: Name the pain. Make the audience feel it.
Solution: Present your fix. Keep it clear and concrete.
Benefit: Paint the better future. What changes if we act?

Natural for anyone in investing — you already think in terms of problems and solutions.

AREV — Answer, Reason, Evidence, Verdict

Best for: Q&A sessions, defending a position, interviews
1 Answer
2 Reason
3 Evidence
4 Verdict
How to use AREV:
Answer: Give a direct answer immediately. Don't hedge.
Reason: Explain your logic.
Evidence: Back it up. Data, experience, or analogy.
Verdict: Summarize your position with confidence.

Similar to PREP but more assertive. Use when you need to sound decisive under pressure.

Quick Tips for Sounding Smart on the Spot

  • Buy time without sounding like you're stallingUse bridge phrases: "That's a great question — the way I think about it is..." or "Let me frame this in terms of..." These give you 3-5 seconds to organize your thoughts.
  • Lead with your conclusionSay your main point first, then support it. This is the opposite of how we naturally think (build up to a point), but it sounds far more confident and structured.
  • Use the "Rule of Three"Group your thoughts into three points. "There are three things to consider here..." Three is memorable, structured, and sounds complete — even if you're making it up as you go.
  • Acknowledge what you don't know"I'm not deeply familiar with X, but here's how I'd think about it from a [your expertise] perspective..." Honesty + reframing = instant credibility.
  • Use analogies aggressivelyIf you don't know the specifics, compare it to something you do know. "This is similar to how..." Analogies make you sound thoughtful and help the audience follow along.
  • Pause instead of fillingA 2-second pause sounds powerful and deliberate. "Um" and "uh" sound uncertain. When you feel a filler word coming, just stop talking briefly. The silence feels longer to you than to the audience.
  • End strong — don't trail offPlan your last sentence. Drop your vocal pitch at the end (signals authority). Never end with "so, yeah" or "that's pretty much it." Instead: "And that's why I believe X."
  • Reframe questions you can't answer"I think the more interesting question is..." or "The way I'd reframe that is..." This lets you steer toward what you do know.
  • Speak in shorter sentencesLong, winding sentences signal that you're figuring it out as you go. Short sentences signal clarity. When in doubt, put a period where you'd put a comma.
  • Practice the "one breath" testIf you can't say your main point in one breath, it's not clear enough. Simplify until you can. Complexity is easy; clarity is hard — and impressive.

Mental Priming

Read this visualization slowly. It activates the same neural pathways as actually speaking well.

Picture yourself walking to the front of a room. You feel calm. You make eye contact with someone in the audience and smile. The question comes. You pause — just a beat. You hear yourself start with a clear, confident opening. Your voice is steady, your pace is measured. You use a specific example that makes the room nod. You land your closing line and stop cleanly. The room is impressed — not because you were perfect, but because you were clear, structured, and present.

Power Poses (30 seconds)

Stand up. Feet shoulder-width apart. Hands on hips or arms wide. Hold for 30 seconds. Research by Carney & Cuddy shows this reduces cortisol and increases testosterone — even if you don't believe it works. At minimum, it forces you out of a hunched, phone-scrolling posture before you speak.

Vocal Range Exercise

Read this sentence aloud 3 times, each time with a different emphasis. This trains vocal variety so you don't sound monotone.

"I didn't say we should drop the deal."

1st time: emphasize "I" (someone else said it)
2nd time: emphasize "say" (I implied it)
3rd time: emphasize "drop" (I said we should restructure it)
